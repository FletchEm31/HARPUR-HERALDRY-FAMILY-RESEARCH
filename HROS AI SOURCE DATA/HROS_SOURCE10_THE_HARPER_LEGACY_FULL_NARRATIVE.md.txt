I. THE NORMAN NAME AND THE LORDS OF RUSHALL
The story begins not in England but in Normandy, France, where the name Harpour — a Norman French
occupational name for a player of the harp — crossed the English Channel with William the Conqueror in
1066. Over the following centuries the family established themselves as landed gentry in the English
Midlands, their name gradually anglicising from le Harpur to Harpur as they put down roots in Staffordshire.
The Rushall line itself traces back to 1065 AD — predating the Conquest — making this one of the oldest
documented gentry families in England.
By the early 15th century the family had risen to prominence under Sir John Harpur, Lord of Rushall,
Staffordshire — confirmed by the History of Parliament Online and TNA primary source SC8/248/12368.
Sir John served as Member of Parliament for Stafford from 1419 to 1431, Auditor-General to the Duke of
Buckingham, Escheator, Justice of the Peace, and Commissioner for Staffordshire. He acquired the lordship of
Rushall through his marriage to Eleanor, daughter and heir of William Grober of Rushall, and added multiple
Essex manors from November 1422.
Sir John died 3 July 1464 and was buried at Grey Friars, Lichfield. He left two sons — William and Richard.
This is where the story divides.
The Division at Rushall
William — the eldest — inherited Rushall and continued the main Staffordshire line, from which the Harpur
Baronets of Calke Abbey ultimately descend through a cadet branch. Richard — the younger son — became
Receiver-General of the Duchy of Lancaster and founded the Essex branch whose story this document tells.

1386-1421) states explicitly: "He left two sons, William and Richard." Henry appears only in the baronetcy's
own internal pedigree (Stebbing's MS) as a claimed intermediary within William's line — not as a third son

II. THE ROYAL FINANCIER — SIR RICHARD HARPUR II OF EPPING
Sir Richard Harpur II established himself at Latton Hall, Essex, acquiring the estate in 1486 through his
marriage to Dame Elizabeth, daughter and co-heir of Sir Piers Arderne. He was Receiver-General to the
Dukes of Buckingham, Crown Servant to King Richard III, and Receiver-General for Lady Margaret Beaufort
— mother of the future Henry VII.
This last detail is remarkable. Sir Richard served three successive and often opposing powers during the Wars
of the Roses without losing his head or his estate. Managing the finances of a duke, a king, and the mother of a
rival king simultaneously, across a period of dynastic civil war, requires either extraordinary political genius
or a level of financial indispensability that made him untouchable. Possibly both.
His will — proved at the Prerogative Court of Canterbury, the highest probate court in England — contains a
specific bequest to the Church of Rushall, Staffordshire, 150 miles from his Essex home. A man does not
leave money to a church 150 miles distant without a direct family connection. This bequest is primary source
confirmation that the Essex Harper line and the Derbyshire Harpur Baronet line share a common Rushall
origin. Sir Richard died in 1492 and was buried inside St Mary-at-Latton Church — a privilege reserved for
significant gentry. He left two sons: Richard and John.

III. THE TUDOR COURTIER — SIR GEORGE HARPER AND THE
QUEEN'S SCANDAL
Richard Harper of Latton — the elder son — inherited Latton Hall and married Constance Chamberlayne,
daughter of Sir Robert Chamberlayne, Knight of the Bath, Knight of the King's Body, and Member of
Parliament. Sir Robert held estates at Capel St Mary and Gedding in Suffolk — both within 10 miles of
Boxford. Richard died on 10 April 1509, leaving one confirmed son: Sir George Harper.

Shot at Boulogne — and Survived
In the summer of 1544, Henry VIII launched one of the last great English military campaigns of his reign —
the siege and conquest of Boulogne, France. George Harper crossed the Channel with the English army. After
Boulogne fell in July 1544, he remained behind to organise the logistics of the occupation — specifically the
transport of sick and wounded soldiers back to England. It was unglamorous, essential work — and it nearly
killed him.
During the Boulogne campaign, George Harper took a gunshot wound. This detail — confirmed in the
History of Parliament Online — is extraordinary for its time. Firearms were still relatively new on the
European battlefield. A matchlock arquebus fired a lead ball capable of catastrophic injury. That George
Harper survived a gunshot wound in 1544 — without modern medicine, without antibiotics, on a foreign
campaign — and lived another 14 years to die in his bed at the Blackfriars in London in 1558 — is a testament
to either remarkable physical resilience or extraordinary luck. Probably both. The 3rd Duke of Norfolk
commended him personally for his service — one of the most powerful men in England acknowledging a
Kent knight who had just survived being shot.
The Queen's Scandal — Thomas Culpeper
After Richard Harper's death in 1509, Constance Chamberlayne remarried Alexander Colepeper. Their son
— George Harper's half-brother — was Thomas Culpeper, Gentleman of the Privy Chamber to Henry VIII,
trusted enough to sleep at the foot of the King's bed.
In 1541, Thomas Culpeper was accused of conducting an affair with Catherine Howard — Henry VIII's fifth
wife and Queen of England, aged approximately 19. Whether the affair was consummated or purely emotional
remains historically debated. What is certain is the outcome: Culpeper was beheaded at Tyburn in December
1541. Catherine Howard was beheaded at the Tower of London in February 1542.
George Harper survived the fallout — receiving some of Culpeper's attainted lands including the Manor of
Penshurst. He was later imprisoned in the Tower during Wyatt's Rebellion in 1554 and fined heavily. He died
7 December 1558 at the Blackfriars, London, and was buried 12 December 1558 at St Martin's, Ludgate —
with two heralds of arms present and a crest of a gilt boar — the confirmed Harpur family crest, recorded in
the 1574 Kent Visitation (Harleian Society Vol.74, p.62) and Henry Machyn's Diary (p.181, 1848). He died
without male heirs — extinguishing the Essex main line entirely.

IV. THE WARS THEY SURVIVED
The Harper and Harpur families navigated one of the most violent and politically unstable periods in English
history — and somehow came out the other side intact. Here is what they survived:
The Wars of the Roses (1455–1487)
Sir Richard Harpur II of Epping served the Yorkist Dukes of Buckingham, the Yorkist King Richard III, and
the Lancastrian Lady Margaret Beaufort — all without losing his head. The Wars of the Roses killed two
kings, multiple dukes, and destroyed dozens of noble and gentry families who backed the wrong side. Sir
Richard backed everyone simultaneously and survived.
The Tudor Succession Crises (1509–1558)
George Harper lived through six monarchs in 55 years — Henry VII, Henry VIII, Edward VI, Lady Jane
Grey (nine days), Mary I, and Elizabeth I. Each succession brought new dangers. He was imprisoned in the
Tower under Mary I, fined heavily, connected to an executed royal favourite, survived a gunshot wound in
France, and walked out alive each time. When asked to retain 30 armed men to support Lady Jane Grey's
claim in 1553, he hedged his bets perfectly — surviving Mary I's accession without catastrophic
consequences.
The Dissolution of the Monasteries (1536–1541)
Rather than being destroyed by the Dissolution, George Harper profited from it — receiving the house and site
of the dissolved priory of Austin Friars in Canterbury plus five Essex manors as grants. He sold most within
15 months but the windfall confirmed his ability to turn political upheaval into personal advantage.
Wyatt's Rebellion (1554)
Sir Thomas Wyatt the Younger led a Protestant uprising against Queen Mary's proposed marriage to Philip of
Spain. George Harper was implicated — imprisoned in the Tower, released on recognizances of £4,000, fined
lands worth £100 or £2,000 cash, called before the Privy Council. He survived this too.
The English Civil War (1642–1651)
By this point the family had split into two lines. The Calke baronetcy line — Sir John Harpur of Swarkestone
— was fined £4,583 during Civil War composition proceedings, reduced to £4,000. This implies an estate
worth tens of thousands of pounds and confirms the family backed the Royalist cause. They survived the
Parliamentarian victory and retained Calke Abbey. The Suffolk branch — James Harbar of Beccles and his
descendants — were entirely untouched, quietly farming and trading in East Anglia while England tore itself
apart.
John Harper's disappearance into Suffolk in 1509 had placed his descendants in exactly the right place to survive
everything that followed.
V. THE VANISHING SON — JOHN HARPER AND THE SUFFOLK
MYSTERY
Here is where the story becomes extraordinary.
Sir Richard Harpur II's will (PROB 11/9/246) names his younger son explicitly: Johannem Harpur filio meo
— 'John Harpur my son' — assigned Essex lands at Stanford and Stapulford contingent on his brother Richard
dying without male heirs. Richard died 10 April 1509. The contingency clause activated. John Harper
inherited. And then John Harper vanishes from the historical record.
He appears once more — in the 1574 Kent Visitation (Harleian Society Vol.74, p.62), recorded as 'John
Harpur 1 sonne' under his father Richard in the official Harpur pedigree. No wife recorded. No children
recorded. No location. Just a name — and then silence.
A Deliberate Disappearance?
Consider the context. John Harper inherited his Essex lands in 1509 with no manor house, no established seat,
no court connections of his own. His sister-in-law Constance had remarried into the Colepeper family. Over
the following decades, as John quietly established himself in Suffolk under a phonetically corrupted surname,
the Colepeper connection became one of the most dangerous associations in England.
By 1541 — when Thomas Culpeper was executed for allegedly sleeping with the Queen — John Harper had
been in Suffolk for approximately 30 years, known as Harbertt, recorded in no heraldic visitation, connected
to no court, invisible to Tudor investigators. Whether this was a deliberate family arrangement or simply
fortunate timing, the result was identical. John Harper ceased to exist. A man named John Harbertt appeared
in Boxford, Suffolk, around 1510.
The geographic logic is compelling. Constance Chamberlayne's father Sir Robert held estates at Capel St
Mary (8.5 miles from Boxford) and Gedding (10 miles from Boxford). A younger son with no Essex
inheritance, gravitating toward his sister-in-law's family network in Suffolk, settling almost exactly between
those two estates — this is not coincidence. This is a man who knew where he was going.

VII. THE NAME COMES HOME
In 1733 — 224 years after John Harper disappeared into Suffolk — a man named James Harper married
Sarah Osborn at Stoke-by-Nayland. Not Harbar. Not Harboe. Not Harbor. Harper. The name had returned.
Whether by conscious decision, inherited family knowledge, or the natural completion of a phonetic cycle —
by 1733 the family called themselves Harper again. In 1737, James Harper baptised his son James Harper at
Nayland, Suffolk. In 1765, that James Harper's son Joseph Harper crossed the Atlantic to New York.
The name that was hidden for 200 years came home just in time to cross an ocean.
VIII. AMERICA — FROM A PRINTING PRESS TO A PUBLISHING
EMPIRE
Joseph Harper settled in New York in 1765, carrying a name that had survived Tudor court scandals, phonetic
corruption across eight counties, 200 years of Suffolk dialect, and the pre-registration darkness that swallowed
entire gentry families without trace. His sons would build one of the most consequential publishing empires in
American history.
In 1817, brothers James and John Harper founded Harper \& Brothers in New York City — beginning
with a small printing operation on Dover Street, Lower Manhattan. Their two brothers Wesley and Fletcher
joined the firm, and Harper \& Brothers became a family dynasty in the truest sense — four brothers building
an empire from scratch, carrying a name that had been hidden for 200 years in a Suffolk village. By the 1830s
they were the largest printing firm in the United States.
Harper's Weekly and the Election of Abraham Lincoln
In 1857, Harper \& Brothers launched Harper's Weekly — a national illustrated newspaper that would
become the most influential periodical in America, reaching over 200,000 readers weekly. When Abraham
Lincoln ran for president in 1860, Harper's Weekly became one of his most powerful advocates. Lincoln
himself called Harper's Weekly 'the greatest recruiter for the United States Military.' The magazine's editorial
support, combined with its extraordinary national reach, helped shape public opinion across the Northern
states at a moment when the country was fracturing over slavery. The Suffolk Harpers helped elect the
president who abolished it.
The Elephant and the Donkey
The magazine's star illustrator was Thomas Nast — recruited by Fletcher Harper in 1862 and given
extraordinary editorial freedom. It was through Harper's Weekly that Nast introduced two of the most
enduring symbols in American political history. In 1874, Nast published a cartoon featuring a donkey
representing the Democratic Party. Shortly afterward he introduced the elephant as the symbol of the
Republican Party. Both images first appeared in Harper's Weekly. Both symbols have defined American
politics for 150 years. They came from a Suffolk farming family whose ancestor disappeared from Essex in
1509.
The Titanic — A Harper Survives Again
The Harper family's American story includes one of the most famous disasters in history. Henry Sleeper
Harper — a direct relative in the American Harper line and great-grandson of Fletcher Harper — was aboard
the RMS Titanic when it struck an iceberg and sank on 15 April 1912, killing over 1,500 people.
Henry Sleeper Harper survived. He escaped in Lifeboat 3 — one of the first lifeboats launched — along with
his wife, his personal guide Hamad Hassab, and his Pekingese dog named Sun Yat-sen. His survival was
noted in newspapers across America. The dog's survival caused particular public comment — lifeboats were
desperately overcrowded and hundreds of people died while a Pekingese escaped.
It was not the first time a Harper had survived against the odds. George Harper survived a gunshot wound at
Boulogne in 1544. John Harper survived the most dangerous political period in Tudor history by disappearing
entirely. Henry Sleeper Harper survived the Titanic. This family has an extraordinary habit of coming through.
His survival meant the Harper publishing dynasty continued — and HarperCollins, the direct corporate
descendant of Harper \& Brothers, publishes to this day as one of the five largest publishing companies on
earth.
IX. THE CALCULATED MAN — RICHARD'S DEATHBED PLAN
Of all the extraordinary figures in the Harper lineage, none demonstrates more raw intelligence than Sir
Richard Harpur II of Epping — the man who may have engineered his family's survival from his deathbed.
Consider his career first. As Receiver-General to the Dukes of Buckingham, Crown Servant to King Richard
III, and Receiver-General for Lady Margaret Beaufort — mother of the future Henry VII — Sir Richard
maintained the complete financial trust of every major faction in English politics simultaneously during the
Wars of the Roses. This was not luck. The Wars of the Roses was a 30-year civil war in which entire noble
families were attainted, executed, and erased for backing the wrong side. Richard backed every side. And lost
nothing.
To do this required something rare even among the most accomplished Tudor courtiers — the ability to make
yourself so financially indispensable that your elimination became unthinkable regardless of who held power.
The men whose money he managed trusted him with their most sensitive financial information. That trust was
his armour. He wore it through three successive regimes without a scratch.
Then he died young — approximately 39 years old — leaving a six year old son.
The Deathbed Calculation
Richard in 1509 could see the landscape with perfect clarity. His son George was about to be absorbed into the
Colepeper household through wardship — connected to one of the most politically aggressive families at the
Tudor court. Richard knew what that world did to people. He had spent his entire career navigating it.
He also had a younger brother — John — with no estate, no seat, no mapped future. A younger son with the
Stanford and Stapulford lands as his only inheritance and the whole dangerous world of Essex court politics
surrounding him.
The deathbed calculation would have been cold and precise: George is going into the court world. I cannot
stop that. Colepeper will raise him and the court will shape him. But John — John I can protect. And through
John I can protect the family name and the family line if George's world turns against him.
Get out of Essex. Go to Suffolk. Settle near Constance's family estates at Capel St Mary and Gedding. Be far enough
away that nobody connects you to whatever happens at court. And whatever you do — remember who you are.
Degeneranti Genus Opprobrium — 'To degenerate from one's ancestors is a disgrace.' The Harpur family motto,
confirmed on the Harpur Crewe coat of arms, c.1812 (National Trust Collections, Calke Abbey).
The Stanford and Stapulford lands were not simply an inheritance. They were seed capital — startup money
for a new life in a county where nobody was watching, and the name Harper could quietly become Harbertt
without anyone caring enough to investigate.
Then Everything Richard Feared Came True
Exactly as he had calculated it might. George ends up raised by the Colepepers. Thomas Culpeper — his
half-brother — is executed for allegedly sleeping with the Queen of England. George is shot in France.
George is imprisoned in the Tower. George is fined £4,000. George dies without male heirs and the entire
Essex estate disperses. The court world destroyed everything around George — repeatedly, systematically,
almost completely.
And John? Safely in Suffolk. Under a different name. Keeping the family line alive while his nephew
navigated disaster after disaster at the highest levels of Tudor power. The line Richard had quietly moved out
of danger in 1509 survived everything the 16th century threw at it — because one dying man understood
power well enough to know when to walk away.
The Name — The Final Proof
Richard didn't just tell John to disappear. He told him to remember who he was. That instruction — passed
down through six generations of Suffolk farmers and tradespeople, through Harberds and Harbars and
Harboes and Harbers — survived intact for 224 years. In 1733, James Harper wrote the name in a Suffolk
parish register exactly as his ancestor had carried it out of Essex. Not Herbert. Not Harbour. Not Harber.
Harper.
A dying man's instruction. Two centuries of faithful memory. One name restored just in time to cross an
ocean. Sir Richard Harpur II of Epping navigated the Wars of the Roses without losing a single asset, served
three mutually hostile factions without losing the trust of any of them, and from his deathbed engineered his
family's survival across a political catastrophe he never lived to see. His plan worked for 224 years.
The Destination — Hidden in Plain Sight
If the deathbed plan required John to disappear — Richard chose the destination with the same cold precision
he applied to everything else. He did not send his brother to hide in poverty in some forgotten Norfolk
fenland. He sent him to one of the wealthiest and most established parts of England.
Bury St Edmunds in the early 16th century was not a random Suffolk town. It was home to the Abbey of St
Edmund — one of the wealthiest and most powerful Benedictine monasteries in medieval England, a major
pilgrimage site drawing wealth from across the country, and a significant legal and administrative centre.
Boxford sat in the heart of the Suffolk wool belt — the wealthiest agricultural region in England at the time
— surrounded by prosperous cloth-working towns. Lavenham, just six miles away, was at this exact period
one of the wealthiest towns per capita in all of England, built entirely on the wool trade.
A poor man does not arrive in Boxford. A landless stranger does not establish himself as a patriarch with two
sons marrying in consecutive years at the same parish. You arrive in Boxford in 1510 with one of two things
— existing family connections that smooth your entry into the community, or capital that earns you immediate
gentry standing. John Harbertt had both.
The Chamberlayne family connections — Sir Robert's estates at Capel St Mary and Gedding, both within 10
miles of Boxford — gave him the social introduction. The Stanford and Stapulford land inheritance gave him
the capital. He did not arrive as a stranger. He arrived as a man expected.
The Gentry Network He Immediately Entered
Within one generation the Boxford Harberds were connected to the armigerous Burwell family of Sutton —
confirmed in the Suffolk Visitation 1612 — the Smythe family through John Harbarde's marriage to Rose
Smythe in 1563, and the wider Harberd gentry network including Francis Harberd holding 200 acres across
Long Melford, Ufford and Bredfield. These are not the connections of a man who arrived poor and unknown.
These are the connections of a man who arrived with standing and was immediately recognised as belonging
to the gentry class.
By 1669 the family had a 10 hearth property in Beccles — confirmed by the Suffolk Hearth Tax (TNA
E179/257/17). Ten hearths is gentry level. That wealth does not accumulate in three generations from nothing.
It accumulates from a starting point of capital and connection.
The Probability of Coincidence
Consider what you would need to believe if John Harbertt of Boxford had nothing to do with Richard's brother
John Harper. You would need to accept that a completely unrelated man — using a phonetic variant of Harper
— appeared in Boxford within 50 years of John Harper inheriting the Stanford and Stapulford lands. That he
settled within 10 miles of the exact estates belonging to Sir Robert Chamberlayne. That he arrived with
sufficient capital and connections to immediately establish himself at gentry level. That his descendants
inexplicably reverted the name to Harper 224 years later. And that all of this had nothing to do with the most
politically calculated man in the family — a man who served three hostile factions simultaneously without
losing anything — somehow failing to plan for his family's survival when he knew he was dying at 39.
Every single one of those coincidences would need to be true simultaneously. The probability of that is essentially
zero.
Richard did not send John to hide in poverty. He sent him to thrive — quietly, safely, and in exactly the right
place. In plain sight. In the wealthiest part of Suffolk. Exactly where his sister-in-law's family already had
roots. With enough capital to establish himself immediately. With a name that would corrupt naturally through
dialect without any deliberate disguise required. And with one instruction that survived 224 years intact.
Degeneranti Genus Opprobrium — 'To degenerate from one's ancestors is a disgrace.' The Harpur family motto,
confirmed on the Harpur Crewe coat of arms, c.1812 (National Trust Collections, Calke Abbey).
Note: The deathbed conversation between Richard and John is reasoned inference from confirmed primary sources — not a documented
record. Every building block is sourced. The interpretation is the authors'.
The Baronetcy Line Had the Same Problem
Here is perhaps the most compelling legal argument in the entire claim — and it comes directly from the
baronetcy's own pedigree.
The Harpur of Calke pedigree — the same document that records the creation of the baronetcy line —
contains the following admission about its own ancestry. Describing the descent from Sir John of Rushall to
Richard Harpur of Swarkestone, the text states explicitly: 'Henry, was father (or grandfather) of Richard
Harpur, Esq. one of the Justices of the Common Pleas.'
NOTE: Henry here is not a third son of Sir John of Rushall — that claim is disproven by History of
Parliament (HC 1386-1421). Henry is an intermediary figure within William's line — the baronetcy's own
internal descent from William (eldest son of John of Rushall) through to Richard the Judge. The baronetcy
could not confirm whether Henry was one or two generations removed from Richard the Judge who died in
1573. That is not a minor qualification buried in a footnote. That is a formal admission of a generational gap
in the official pedigree of a hereditary title — recorded in the document used to support the baronetcy
creation itself.
The Same Window. The Same Problem.
Consider the timing of that uncertainty:
Sir John of Rushall dies 1464. William — eldest son — inherits Rushall. Henry — an intermediary within
William's line — active post 1464. Richard Harpur of Swarkestone the Judge dies 1573. That is a 109 year
gap within the baronetcy's own line — with one confirmed name in between — Henry — and no primary
source document explicitly connecting Henry to Richard the Judge.
The Harper Suffolk line gap runs from John Harper vanishing in 1509 to John Harbertt appearing at Boxford
c.1510-1540. That is a maximum of 30 years. Against the baronetcy line's 109 year uncertainty.
Both gaps fall in the same historical window — roughly 1464 to 1540 — the pre-registration period before
Thomas Cromwell's 1538 parish register mandate. Both involve the same problem: a generation or two of
gentry activity occurring entirely before systematic parish registration, where the only surviving records are
wills, Chancery proceedings, Inquisitions Post Mortem, and heraldic visitations — all incomplete and patchy
for this period.
The Precedent Is Already Set
The College of Arms accepted the baronetcy pedigree with that 'father or grandfather' uncertainty intact —
and on the strength of it created a hereditary title in 1626. They granted a baronetcy on a pedigree that could
not confirm whether a key ancestor was one or two generations removed from the proven chain.

X. HOW THE BARONETCY WAS WON — AND LOST
The Harpur Baronetcy of Calke Abbey was not inherited — it was purchased. In 1626, King Charles I,
desperate to raise funds without convening Parliament, instituted the sale of baronetcies at a fixed price of
approximately £1,095. It was effectively a financial transaction dressed in royal ceremony — the Crown
needed money, ambitious gentry families wanted titles, and a new class of hereditary knights was created
almost overnight.
Sir Henry Harpur of Calke — third son of Sir John Harpur of Swarkestone, Knight — was created the 1st
Baronet of Calke Abbey on 8 December 1626, in the second year of King Charles I's reign. He paid for the
privilege. But the title was real, the patent was sealed, and the Harpur family — descendants of the same Sir
John of Rushall whose younger son Richard had founded the Essex branch — were now formally ennobled.
The baronetcy passed through ten holders across nearly three centuries. The last — Sir Vauncey Harpur
Crewe, 10th Baronet — was one of the most eccentric English aristocrats of the Victorian era. He refused to
allow a railway through his estate, kept Calke Abbey completely unchanged for decades, collected thousands
of natural history specimens, and rarely left his house. When he died in 1924 without male heirs, rooms at
Calke Abbey were found untouched for generations.
The National Trust took over Calke Abbey in 1985 — in lieu of an £8 million inheritance tax bill — and
famously chose to preserve it as a 'house in decline,' displaying the decay rather than restoring it. The house
your family's baronetcy was created to protect ended up preserved as a monument to aristocratic eccentricity.
There is something almost poetic about that.
The Claim — Why It Matters
The title lapsed on Sir Vauncey's death in 1924. It was never formally declared extinct. It is dormant. Under
English law a dormant baronetcy remains claimable by a proven male-line heir.
The Johnson family — the only other known claimants — descend through the female line via Edmund Lewis
Crewe, brother of the 8th Baronet. Under the Letters Patent creating the baronetcy, female-line descent cannot
support a claim. Hayden F. Harper's claim runs through the male line from Richard, second son of
Rushall — making it structurally superior to the Johnson claim under the terms of the original creation.
One gap remains: the primary source document explicitly connecting John Harper (named in PROB 11/9/246
and the 1574 Kent Visitation) to John Harbertt of Boxford (confirmed from 1562 in FB77/D1/1). That gap is
the active research frontier. When it closes — and the evidence strongly suggests it will — the chain from Sir
John Harpur of Rushall to Hayden F. Harper will be complete.
Nine hundred and sixty years. One name. One unbroken line.
